第一段

  1. The speaker and author hold divergent attitudes toward (the topic). Through relevant reasons and examples, the lecturer extensively refutes the [pessimistic] viewpoints stated in the reading material.
  2. The materials focus on (the topic). The author anticipates that (opinion), while the speaker presents his opposite thoughts.
  3. The author discussed (the topic) and considers this [accusation] [frivolous]. However, the speaker displays a contradictory view that (opinion).
  4. The speaker raises doubts on the writer’s deduction that (opinion). According to the lecture, (opinion). This judgment is established on a series of counter evidence.
  5. The passage concentrates on (the topic). But the lecturer demonstrates his disagreement, arguing that (opinion).
  6. The author exhibits his suspicion on (the topic), of which the speaker disapproves. In the lecture, he aims to prove that the article is filled with errors.
  7. This set of materials focus on the debate over (the topic). The lecturer presents the contradictory idea that this [measure] may not be as [beneficial] as the writer conceives.
  8. The writer elaborates [three] theories about (topic target), attempting to solve the mystery how (question). Nevertheless, the lecturer confutes the author’s explanations and labels them as feeble.
  9. The writer holds that (opinion). This hypothesis is retorted by the speaker, who believes that (opinion).
  10. The reading passage centers on the writer’s complaints against (topic target). However, the speaker, who [recognizes their value], presents his refutation in the lecture.

  2. The passage says that (statement A), while the lecturer argues that (statement B). (Further statement B), which contradicts the point made in the passage.
  3. Opposing the author's belief that (statement A), the professor contends that (statement B). Thus, (further statement B).
  4. As the passage declares, (statement A). In contrast, the professor stresses (statement B). Obviously, this forms a sharp contradiction against what is stated by the writer.
  5. The passage maintains that (statement A). Again, this is retorted by the lecture, which asserts that (statement B). Obviously, this forms a sharp contradiction against what is stated by the writer.
  6. The author takes it for granted that (statement A). Yet the professor insists that (statement B), which completely rejects the opinion shown in the passage.
  7. The passage says that (statement A). But the professor challenges this idea by providing the truth that (statement B). Thus, contrary to the writer's notion, (further statement B).
  8. The writer indicates that (statement A). To render disproof, the lecturer reveals a piece of information about (statement B), which further refutes the example in the reading.
  9. The passage puts all the blame on (target A), arguing that (statement A), whereas the lecture contends that (statement B). Consequently, (further statement B), and this overturns the writer's concept.
  10. The article bases its inference on [the discovery] that (statement A), while the lecture argues that (statement B). (Further statement B), This contradicts the assumption in the passage.
  11. The author advocates that (statement A). However, as is rebutted by the speaker, (statement B). In other words, (further statement B), which objects to what the passage suggests.
  12. The lecture challenges the writer's supposition that (statement A). Obviously, (statement B). Thus, (the topic target) must differ from the conclusion drawn by the writer.
  13. the writer claims that (statement A). Nevertheless, the professor firmly opposes the standpoint that (statement B). In fact, (further statement B), thereupon overthrowing the notion of the passage.
  14. the writer regards (topic target) as (description) by citing (statement A), while the lecture refutes this false impression and clarifies the truth that (statement B). Through enumerating their favorable influences on (examples), the professor contends that (further statement B).
  15. As the author puts it, (statement A) . The passage describes how (further statement A). However, the lecturer presents his disapproval by explaining how (statement B).
  16. The passage brings up (evidence), claiming that (statement A). To rebut this point, the professor emphasizes the [historical] fact that (statement B). Consequently, it is understandable (further statement B).
  17. The second problem noted by the writer is that (statement A). However, the professor says that (statement B). This constitutes an effective counterargument against what the passage sets forth.
  18. The article questions why (statement A). Challenging this doubt, the lecturer suggests that (statement B). It is also likely that (further statement B). These all refute the writer's suspicion.
  19. The passage stresses its effectiveness in (statement A). Conversely, the speaker argues that (statement B). Therefore, instead of (statement A), (further statement B), which rebuts the writer's point.
  20. The passage owes (result A) to (target A). In the author's opinion, (statement A). Nonetheless, such a conception is opposed by the professor, who believes that (statement B).
  21. As is asserted by the author, (statement A). He moves on to underline (result A). But the lecturer demonstrates a different attitude with concrete evidence that (statement B). This [sad] fact retorts the [positive] view in the passage.
  22. The passage says that (statement A). To prove the fragility of this theory, the speaker raises the point that (statement B). As for the supposition about(another statement A), it is also retorted in the lecture, because (another statement B).
  23. The second theory describes (statement A). The lecturer argues that (statement B). Yet in most cases, (further statement B), which contradicts the view held by the writer.
  24. The passage infers that (statement A), whereas the lecture rebuts this theory by notifying that (statement B). (Further statement B), and this further refutes what is expressed in the passage.
  25. The passage claims that (statement A). Such a unilateral idea is challenged by the professor’s counterargument that (statement B). (Further statement B).
  26. The writer attributes (result A) to (target A). The professor, however, argues that (statement B). Meanwhile, (further statement B) This contradicts what is discussed in the passage.
